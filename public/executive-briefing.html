About a 5-minute read
The idea in one minute

This was never about booking hotels. It's about what makes a company worth more.

CleanSpace and Allied track labor and materials to the penny. But some of the most important things in the business — how travel gets booked, how a bid gets priced, who knows which vendor to call — live in people's heads and in private spreadsheets. That works fine day to day. The problem shows up on the day you sell.

A buyer pays for what they can see, trust, and keep running after the current people leave. Knowledge that lives in one person's head is the opposite of that — and it quietly holds down the price. The moment that same knowledge lives in software the company owns, it stops being a risk and becomes an asset on the balance sheet.

Kelly built CleanSpace Stay to solve one version of this for her own work — crew travel. It runs today. This briefing uses it as the proof, then lays out the bigger opportunity: doing that same thing, on purpose, across the parts of the business that decide your final sale price.

A person on a spreadsheet is a process. The same thing in software the company owns is an asset. Only one of them shows up in the sale price.
What actually moves the number

Two companies can earn the same profit and sell for very different prices.

Specialty contractors typically sell for somewhere between 3.5 and 7 times earnings (EBITDA). What decides where a company lands in that range usually isn't the work itself — it's how de-risked and transferable the operation looks to a buyer. Almost every one of those factors is an information-and-systems problem.

What a buyer looks at Holds the price down (3–5×) Lifts the price up (5–7×+)
Dependence on key people The operation lives in a few people's heads Documented systems run without them
Margin predictability Cost surprises, true margin known only after a job ends Tight cost tracking, few surprises
Workforce stability Turnover, scramble to staff jobs Retention and smooth crew deployment
Records & data Spreadsheets and folders no one else can follow Clean, electronic records a buyer can trust
Differentiation Looks like every other contractor Owns capability that's hard to copy

Range and drivers reflect published M&A guidance on specialty-contractor valuations. The point isn't the exact multiple — it's that the gap between the low and high end is made of fixable, systems-driven factors.

Own it, don't rent it

A subscription is a cost. Owned software is an asset.

There's an important distinction in how this is built. CleanSpace Stay isn't a monthly software subscription bolted onto the P&L. It runs on infrastructure the company can own outright — which matters in two directions.

While you operate it, there are no per-seat fees draining margin as crews grow, and no outside vendor who controls your data or could disappear. When you sell, owned technology and the data underneath it is a differentiated asset a buyer values — not a rented tool that may not even transfer in the deal. It also complements what you already run: this doesn't replace Procore or your systems of record — it turns your own operational data into owned, exit-ready assets alongside them.

●

No recurring license drag

Scale the crews without scaling software cost. Nothing to cancel, nothing that lapses.

●

The data stays yours

Project spend, vendor pricing, and route costs become private intelligence for bidding and negotiation.

●

It transfers in a sale

Proprietary, owned technology is a line a buyer pays for — a subscription is a liability they price against you.
